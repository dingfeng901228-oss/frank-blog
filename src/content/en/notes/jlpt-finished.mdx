JLPT is finally over.
But the results won't come out for at least another month and a half — and yet I already feel like a deflated ball.
The moment the exam ended, the nerves that had been wound tight for months finally let go. But what followed wasn't relief — it was a hollowness I couldn't quite name. Like I'd been running toward one clear goal for so long that when I finally crossed the finish line, I had no idea which direction to run in next.
Maybe I'd just been holding myself too tightly for too long.
Since the exam ended, I can feel my daily rhythm shifting.
I'm gaming more.
I'm sleeping more.
And the time I spend studying Japanese keeps shrinking.
This makes me a little uneasy. Because I know full well — finishing the exam doesn't mean I've actually mastered the language. If anything, I still have a long way to go. Especially with listening and speaking.
Compared to my classmates who've been working part-time jobs the whole time, the gap is glaring. They might not be better test-takers than me, but they're immersed in a real language environment every single day — their speaking and listening are miles ahead of mine.
Often I can read a sentence and understand it perfectly, but I can't necessarily hear it and understand it. And even when I do catch it by ear, I can't always get it out smoothly in my own words. That's my biggest weakness right now.
So lately I've been thinking: maybe it's time to get a part-time job. Not for the money, but to put myself in a position where I'm forced to speak Japanese every single day — to actually live the language instead of just studying it. After all, language was always meant for communication, not something that only exists on an exam paper.
For now, there's no exam pressure hanging over me. I want to use this window to recalibrate my whole approach to studying. Up until now, I've mostly been studying for the JLPT. Going forward, I want to gradually shift my focus toward listening, speaking, and real-world communication.
I don't just want to be able to read a book or an article — I want to be able to sit down with a Japanese person one day, chat casually, and express my own thoughts freely. I want Japanese to become a language I can actually use, not just one I can pass.
Of course, before any of that, I still hope this JLPT attempt goes well. Passing N2 would be both a confirmation of these past few months of effort, and a new starting point to keep moving forward from.
The exam is over.
But the learning is just getting started.
